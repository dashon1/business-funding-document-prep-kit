#### The 10 Most Requested Documents

Lenders most frequently request these documents, in order of frequency:

1. **Business Tax Returns (2-3 years)**
   - Verifies your reported income with official government records
   - Shows consistency in your financial reporting
   - Demonstrates legitimate business operations

2. **Personal Tax Returns (2-3 years)**
   - Shows your overall financial picture
   - Required for personal guarantee requirements
   - Demonstrates your commitment to the business

3. **Profit & Loss Statement (last 12 months)**
   - Demonstrates income, expenses, and net profit trends
   - Shows the financial performance of your business
   - Reveals patterns and seasonality in your operations

4. **Balance Sheet (current)**
   - Displays your assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time
   - Shows the complete financial position of your business
   - Demonstrates your net worth as a business owner

5. **Business Bank Statements (last 6-12 months)**
   - Shows cash flow patterns and account management
   - Proves the legitimacy of your banking relationships
   - Demonstrates your ability to manage cash effectively

6. **Debt Schedule (all current obligations)**
   - Lists every debt with balances, payments, and terms
   - Shows your existing financial obligations
   - Helps lenders calculate your debt service capacity

7. **Accounts Receivable Aging**
   - Shows money owed to you by customers with payment status
   - Demonstrates the quality of your receivables
   - Reveals your cash collection effectiveness

8. **Accounts Payable Aging**
   - Shows money you owe to vendors and suppliers
   - Demonstrates your payment practices
   - Reveals your relationships with suppliers

9. **Business Plan with Use of Funds**
   - Demonstrates strategy and clear thinking
   - Explains exactly how you'll use the financing
   - Shows lenders you've thought through your plans carefully

## SECTION 3: BANK STATEMENT PREP SHEET

### How to Organize and Present Your Business Bank Statements

---

#### Understanding What Lenders Look For

When a lender reviews your bank statements, they're evaluating five key factors that reveal the true health of your business:

**1. Average Daily Balance**

This is the average amount of money in your account over the statement period. Lenders calculate this by adding up each day's balance and dividing by the number of days.

*What they're looking for:* Higher average balances suggest financial stability. They want to see balances that would cover your proposed monthly loan payment multiple times over.

*Pro tip:* Review your last 12 months of statements and note any months where the average balance dropped significantly. Be prepared to explain these periods with context about what happened and how you resolved it.

**2. Cash Flow Consistency**

Lenders analyze the patterns in your deposits. Consistent, regular deposits from customers suggest reliable revenue. Highly variable deposits raise concerns about income stability.

*What they're looking for:* Predictable deposit patterns that match your described business model. If you invoice clients and they pay on net-30 terms, lenders expect that pattern.

*Pro tip:* Create a deposit pattern analysis showing lenders what "normal" looks like for your business. This helps them understand your cash flow cycle and feel confident in your consistency.

**3. Expense Management**

Lenders look at how you manage expenses. Large, unusual withdrawals or consistent overspending can signal poor financial management.

*What they're looking for:* Expenses that align with your business type and size. Regular, reasonable payments to vendors and suppliers.

*Pro tip:* Categorize your major expenses on a separate sheet. When you can explain where money goes, you look more competent and organized.

**4. Balance Trends**

The overall direction of your balances matters. Growing balances suggest a healthy, profitable business. Declining balances raise concerns.

*What they're looking for:* Either stable balances with sufficient reserves OR growing balances demonstrating business growth.

*Pro tip:* Calculate your month-over-month balance change. If you've grown by $5,000 over the year, highlight this. Lenders love to see businesses that are trending upward.

**5. Red Flags**

Lenders train themselves to spot warning signs. Overdrafts, large unexplained transfers, or suspicious activity can kill your application.

*What to watch for:* Multiple overdrafts, large transfers to unknown parties, consistent low balances, or irregular deposit patterns.

*Pro tip:* If you see red flags in your history, address them proactively. Explain what happened and what you changed. Transparency builds trust.

---

#### Step-by-Step Preparation Process

##### Step 1: Gather Your Statements

Collect the last 12 months of business bank statements. If applying for a smaller loan, 6 months may suffice, but 12 months provides the most comprehensive picture and demonstrates consistency over time.

**Where to get them:**

- Download from your online banking portal (preferred method)
- Request official copies from your bank branch
- Access through your bank's mobile app
- Use your bank's customer service line

**Format considerations:**

- PDF format is preferred for digital applications
- Official bank copies with the bank's logo and letterhead are best
- Avoid screenshots—lenders want authentic documentation
- Ensure all pages are included (statements often span multiple pages)

##### Step 2: Review Each Statement

Before presenting your statements, review them critically. Look for:

**Deposits to highlight:**

- Regular customer payments
- Recurring revenue streams
- Large contracts or one-time payments
- Transfers from savings (shows reserves and financial discipline)

**Items to explain:**

- Large irregular withdrawals
- Transfers to personal accounts
- Large payments to vendors
- Any deposits that might look unusual without context

Create a brief note for each statement explaining any items that might raise questions. Transparency builds trust, and lenders appreciate business owners who anticipate concerns.

##### Step 3: Organize by Date Range

Structure your statement package chronologically, most recent first. Each statement should be complete and include all pages.

**Recommended file naming:**

```
2026_04_Bank_Statement.pdf
2026_03_Bank_Statement.pdf
...
2025_05_Bank_Statement.pdf
```

Use consistent naming conventions that include the month and year. This makes it easy for you and for lenders to reference specific periods.

##### Step 4: Create a Summary Page

Add a one-page summary that lenders can review quickly. This should include:

**Account Overview:**

- Bank name and account number (last 4 digits only for security)
- Account type (checking, savings, money market)
- Average monthly balance
- Highest balance in the period
- Lowest balance in the period
- Total deposits over the period
- Total withdrawals over the period

When your bank statements show healthy patterns, make them obvious. Create a separate highlights page that calls attention to:

#### Common Issues and How to Handle Them

**Issue: Large Irregular Deposits**

Problem: A $50,000 deposit that doesn't match your normal pattern might look suspicious to lenders.

Solution: Add a note explaining the deposit. "Deposit represents payment from ABC Corp for project completion, as outlined in contract dated March 15, 2026."

**Issue: Transfers to Personal Accounts**

Problem: Regular transfers to personal accounts can raise questions about how you compensate yourself and whether you're taking excessive draws.

Solution: Be prepared to explain your compensation structure. Business owners who take regular draws are common—just be ready to show that draws are reasonable, consistent, and documented.

**Issue: Low Balance Periods**

Problem: Your business had a slow month with low balances that might concern lenders.

Solution: Context matters. If you know why (seasonal slowdown, large equipment purchase, tax payment, inventory build), explain it clearly. Lenders understand that businesses have fluctuations. Show them you understand your business cycle.

**Issue: Overdrafts**

Problem: Your statements show one or more overdrafts, which raise red flags about cash management.

Solution: These are concerning to lenders, but not necessarily disqualifying. Be ready to explain what happened and how you fixed the underlying issue. If it was a one-time event with clear causes and you've since improved your processes, you can often overcome this concern.

---

#### Best Practices for Bank Statement Presentation

**Do This:**

- Present complete statements: Include all pages, not just the summary page
- Use official documents: Download directly from your bank
- Organize chronologically: Most recent first
- Add context: Include a summary page and explanatory notes
- Highlight positives: Draw attention to healthy patterns and trends
- Be transparent: Explain anything that might look unusual
- Keep copies: Store both digital and physical backups

**Avoid This:**

- Don't use altered statements: Never edit bank statements
- Don't hide negative items: Address issues directly and proactively
- Don't skip months: Gaps in statements raise red flags
- Don't use illegible copies: Ensure all numbers are clear and readable
- Don't include personal statements: Only show business accounts

Lenders need to understand your complete debt obligations before extending new credit. They calculate your debt service coverage ratio, evaluate your leverage, and assess risk based on your existing obligations.

A comprehensive debt overview demonstrates financial transparency and shows lenders you manage your obligations responsibly. It's better to disclose everything upfront than to have lenders discover hidden debts.

---

## SECTION 8: FOLDER ORGANIZATION GUIDE

### Set Up a System That Makes You Look Professional

---

#### Why This Matters

When you apply for funding, lenders don't just evaluate your business—they evaluate you. Business owners who can produce documents quickly and accurately demonstrate operational competence. Those who scramble for hours looking for statements appear disorganized and potentially risky.

This guide teaches you how to organize your financial documents so you're always ready. A well-organized system takes one weekend to set up and saves countless hours every time you need to pull documents together.

---

#### The Basic Framework

Your document organization system has two components working in parallel:

1. **Physical Files** — Paper documents, printed statements, contracts, and official paperwork
2. **Digital Files** — Electronic copies stored on your computer or cloud storage

Both systems follow the same folder structure, making it easy to find documents whether you're looking at a physical file cabinet or a computer folder.

**Key Principle:** Consistency is everything. Use the same folder names, same structure, and same naming conventions everywhere. When everything has a predictable place, you'll never waste time searching.

---

#### The Master Folder Structure

Create one master folder on your computer named "Business Funding Documents"

Inside that folder, create the following subfolders:

```
Business Funding Documents/
├── 01_Business_Identity/
├── 02_Financial_Statements/
├── 03_Bank_Documents/
├── 04_Debt_Documentation/
├── 05_Revenue_Records/
├── 06_Collateral_Documentation/
├── 07_Personal_Financials/
├── 08_Business_Plan/
├── 09_Tax_Returns/
├── 10_Insurance/
├── 11_Licenses_Permits/
└── 12_Submission_Ready/
```

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Submitting outdated documents**

Lenders want statements from the last 30-90 days, not from six months ago. Update your documents regularly so they're always ready.

**Mistake 2: Missing pages**

Bank statements often span multiple pages. Ensure every page is included in your submission package.

**Mistake 3: Inconsistent business names**

Your legal name, DBA, and bank account name must align across all documents—or be clearly explained. Inconsistencies raise red flags.

**Mistake 4: Forgetting personal guarantees**

If you personally guarantee the loan, include your personal financial documents. Many applications fail because owners forget this critical piece.

**Mistake 5: Unclear use of funds**

"Lump sum" and "working capital" are vague. Be specific about exactly what the money will accomplish and how it will generate returns.

**Mistake 6: Math errors**

Double-check every calculation. Lenders will catch mistakes, and they raise questions about your attention to detail.

#### Pro Tips for Successful Applications

1. **Submit complete packages** — Partial applications get delayed or denied. Include everything.
2. **Tell a coherent story** — Your documents should tell the same story. Consistency builds credibility.
3. **Be proactive** — If something looks unusual, explain it before they ask. Anticipate concerns.
4. **Follow instructions** — Lenders often have specific formatting requirements. Follow them exactly.
5. **Stay organized** — Keep copies of everything you submit. Document your submission.
6. **Follow up** — Call to confirm receipt and check on status. Professional persistence is appreciated.
7. **Be responsive** — Answer lender questions quickly to keep the process moving.
