# Bank Statement Preparation Sheet

**How to Organize and Present Your Business Bank Statements for Maximum Impact**

---

## Why Bank Statements Matter

Bank statements are often the first document a lender reviews. They tell a story about your business: how money flows in, how expenses are managed, and whether your business maintains healthy balances. A well-prepared statement package demonstrates professionalism and helps lenders quickly assess your business health.

This guide helps you present your statements in the best possible light while maintaining complete honesty and transparency.

---

## Understanding What Lenders Look For

### Primary Factors Lenders Evaluate

**Average Daily Balance:** Lenders calculate your average balance over the statement period. Higher average balances suggest financial stability and the ability to cover loan payments. They typically want to see at least 2-3 months of average balances that would cover your proposed monthly payment.

**Cash Flow Patterns:** They look for consistency in deposits. A business with highly variable deposits may be considered higher risk. Regular, predictable deposits from customers suggest stable revenue.

**Expense Management:** Lenders notice large or unusual withdrawals. They want to see that you're managing expenses responsibly and not living beyond your means.

**Minimum Balance Maintenance:** Some lenders have minimum balance requirements. Knowing yours helps you understand which statements present your business best.

**Overdraft History:** Frequent overdrafts signal poor cash management and raise red flags for lenders.

## Step-by-Step Preparation Process

### Step 1: Gather Your Statements

Collect the last 12 months of business bank statements. If applying for a smaller loan, 6 months may suffice, but 12 months provides the most comprehensive picture.

**Where to get them:**
- Download from your online banking portal
- Request official copies from your bank branch
- Access through your bank's mobile app
- Use your bank's customer service line

**Format considerations:**
- PDF format is preferred for digital applications
- Official bank copies with the bank's logo and letterhead are best
- Avoid screenshots—lenders want authentic documentation

---

### Step 2: Review Each Statement

Before presenting your statements, review them critically. Look for:

**Deposits to highlight:**
- Regular customer payments
- Recurring revenue streams
- Large contracts or one-time payments
- Transfers from savings (shows reserves)

**Items to explain:**
- Large irregular withdrawals
- transfers to personal accounts
- Large payments to vendors
- Any deposits that might look unusual

Create a brief note for each statement explaining any items that might raise questions. Transparency builds trust.

---

### Step 3: Organize by Date Range

Structure your statement package chronologically, most recent first. Each statement should be complete and include all pages.

**Recommended organization:**
```
2026_04_Bank_Statement.pdf
2026_03_Bank_Statement.pdf
...
2025_05_Bank_Statement.pdf
```

Use consistent naming conventions that include the month and year. This makes it easy for you and for lenders to reference specific periods.

---

### Step 4: Create a Summary Page

Add a one-page summary that lenders can review quickly. This should include:

**Account Overview:**
- Bank name and account number (last 4 digits only for security)
- Account type (checking, savings, money market)
- Average monthly balance
- Highest balance in the period
- Lowest balance in the period

**Monthly Deposit Summary:**
- Total deposits per month
- Number of deposits per month
- Largest single deposit
- Average deposit size

**Key Observations:**
- Consistent monthly revenue
- Seasonal patterns (if applicable)
- Growing balance trends
- Strong cash reserves

---

### Step 5: Highlight Positive Patterns

When your bank statements show healthy patterns, make them obvious. Create a separate highlights page that calls attention to:

**Positive Trend #1: Growing Balances**
"Over the past 12 months, our average monthly balance has grown from $15,000 to $28,000, demonstrating consistent business growth and improved cash management."

**Positive Trend #2: Stable Revenue**
"Monthly deposits average $22,000 with consistent patterns, indicating stable customer payment behavior and reliable revenue streams."

**Positive Trend #3: Cash Reserves**
"Our lowest balance this year was $18,500, well above our operating expenses, demonstrating healthy cash reserves and financial stability."

## Common Issues and How to Handle Them

### Issue: Large Irregular Deposits

**Problem:** A $50,000 deposit that doesn't match your normal pattern might look suspicious.

**Solution:** Add a note explaining the deposit. "Deposit represents payment from ABC Corp for project completion, as outlined in contract dated March 15, 2026."

### Issue: Transfers to Personal Accounts

**Problem:** Regular transfers to personal accounts can raise questions about how you compensate yourself.

**Solution:** Be prepared to explain your compensation structure. Business owners who take regular draws are common—just be ready to show that draws are reasonable and consistent.

### Issue: Low Balance Periods

**Problem:** Your business had a slow month with low balances.

**Solution:** Context matters. If you know why (seasonal slowdown, large equipment purchase, tax payment), explain it. Lenders understand that businesses have fluctuations.

### Issue: Overdrafts

**Problem:** Your statements show one or more overdrafts.

**Solution:** These are concerning to lenders. Be ready to explain what happened and how you fixed the underlying issue. If it was a one-time event with clear causes, you can often overcome this concern.

## Best Practices for Bank Statement Presentation

### Do This

- **Present complete statements:** Include all pages, not just the summary
- **Use official documents:** Download directly from your bank
- **Organize chronologically:** Most recent first
- **Add context:** Include a summary page and explanatory notes
- **Highlight positives:** Draw attention to healthy patterns
- **Be transparent:** Explain anything that might look unusual
- **Keep copies:** Store both digital and physical backups

### Avoid This

- **Don't use altered statements:** Never edit bank statements
- **Don't hide negative items:** Address issues directly
- **Don't skip months:** Gaps in statements raise red flags
- **Don't use illegible copies:** Ensure all numbers are clear
- **Don't include personal statements:** Only show business accounts

## Additional Documentation to Pair with Statements

Bank statements are stronger when paired with supporting documentation:

**For deposit verification:**
- Monthly revenue summary from your accounting system
- List of major customers and payment amounts
- Contracts or invoices for large payments

**For expense verification:**
- Monthly expense breakdown
- Large vendor payment details
- Tax payment records

**For cash flow context:**
- Accounts receivable aging report
- Upcoming payment obligations
- Seasonal revenue patterns
